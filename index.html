Designing a Client–Social Worker Communication App for a Switzerland-Based Organization

PocketM8 is a mobile application designed for clients of social workers to communicate, receive tasks, manage events, and share files in a safe and structured way.

The product is owned by Sozial Digital, a Switzerland-based company serving German-speaking users.

The project involved redesigning and refining an existing app, improving usability, reducing confusion, and making the experience intuitive for users with varying levels of digital literacy.

  • My Role: UX/UI Designer
  • Duration: Initial Design + 5-day Usability & Iteration Cycle
  • Platform: Mobile App (Client Side)
  • Target Users: Clients of social workers (German speaking, Switzerland)

Problem Statement

The existing PocketM8 app functioned well technically, but usability gaps became evident after real-world usage:

  • Multiple login methods caused role confusion (clients vs mentors)
  • Task and event states were not visually clear
  • File interactions lacked clarity (download behavior)
  • Interaction patterns (long press actions, confirmations) were inconsistent
  • Dense layouts reduced readability for non tech-savvy users

Because the app is used by clients who may already be under cognitive or emotional load, clarity and predictability were critical.

Users & Context

Target Audience

  • Clients working with social workers
  • German speaking users
  • Mixed digital literacy levels
  • Often interacting with tasks related to health, administration, or daily life

Usage Context

  • High importance of clarity and trust
  • Users may open the app under stress
  • Actions like accepting or rejecting tasks must feel safe and reversible

Research & Discovery

Sources of Insight

  • Existing wireframes and rough specifications
  • Review of live app behavior
  • Hands-on testing using real flows
  • Direct feedback from the product team
  • Observations from real usage scenarios

Rather than theoretical research, this project relied on practical usability testing, which was crucial given the sensitivity of the user base.

Usability Testing (Live App)

I tested the live production app by simulating both roles:

  • Social worker (web app)
  • Client (mobile app)

Tested Flows

  • SMS login
  • Email login
  • Magic link login
  • Messaging
  • Tasks & events (accept, reject, complete)
  • File upload / download
  • Profile & avatar editing

This revealed real interaction friction that could not be identified in wireframes alone.

Design Principles Applied

  • Clarity over cleverness
  • One action = one outcome
  • Consistent interaction patterns
  • Low cognitive load
  • Accessible visual hierarchy

Design Process

Step 1: Testing & Documentation

  • Tested all major flows
  • Documented friction points directly in Figma
  • Flagged UX vs technical issues

Step 2: Refinement & Redesign

  • Improved layouts, spacing, and alignment
  • Introduced consistent card patterns
  • Refined micro interactions
  • Reduced ambiguity in states and actions

Step 3: Feedback & Iteration

  • Two rounds of structured feedback
  • Adjustments based on product and user insights
  • Final polish for developer-ready handoff

Key Screens Designed

Authentication

  • SMS login
  • Magic link login

Chat & Communication

  • Clear message hierarchy
  • Task and event cards inside chat
  • Predictable interaction behavior

Tasks & Events

  • Visual task states
  • Accept / Reject / Complete logic
  • No duplicate actions leading to same result

Files

  • Clear download affordances
  • Consistent long-press interactions

Profile

  • Clear information grouping
  • Improved visual separation

Outcome & Impact

  • Improved clarity across all task and event states
  • Reduced user confusion in login flows
  • More predictable and accessible interactions
  • Cleaner UI aligned with real user behavior

Learnings

  • Real usability issues appear only in live testing
  • Small wording changes can prevent major confusion
  • Consistency builds trust especially for vulnerable users
  • Designing for social impact requires extra empathy
